special report

Rents have increased more than 10% the past 12 months

Forecasted labor growth for the Seattle market is over 50% higher than the national projections

The population growth in the city has doubled the national average in the past 10 years.

At this time last year, rents in the city of Seattle had hit their lowest levels throughout the pandemic. Over the course of the 12 months since, rents have increased more than 10%, back to pre-pandemic levels. Economists anticipate that 2022 will be a phenomenal year for multifamily real estate and that the United States as a whole has a long runway for growth. Today we look at the Seattle market and why many also believe that the Puget Sound region has a road paved for an even greater boom coming out of the pandemic.

One of the greatest driving forces for the Seattle market over the last decade has been our dynamic employment market fueled by a specialized tech and life sciences workforce. Seattle outpaced the United States in job growth over the last decade and the trend is projected to continue. The current 5-year forecasted labor growth for the Seattle market is 1.1%, over 50% higher than the national projections of 0.7%. With over 1,500,000sf of office currently under construction and a few million more SF in the permitting pipeline, there are big bets in place that Seattle will continue on its path of exponential growth.

Considering the high demand for human capital in the Seattle market, it comes as no surprise that the population growth in the city has doubled the national average in the past 10 years. With many employers continuing to move in from other markets and existing businesses expanding, these population growth trends are likely to persist.

The formula of a strong employment market driving population growth, wages and housing demand has created the foundation for success in the Seattle market. As we emerge from the COVID-19 pandemic, these characteristics are as present as ever and the reason that the Seattle’s rental market has and will continue to increase at rates greater than what is experienced elsewhere in the country.
